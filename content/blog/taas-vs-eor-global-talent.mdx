When global companies decide to hire talent outside their home country, they face a structural question: **how do we engage this person legally, efficiently, and at scale?**

Two models dominate the conversation: **Technology-as-a-Service (TaaS)** and **Employer-of-Record (EOR)**. In my research work at Logictive Solutions — where I've evaluated both models across client contexts in the UK, USA, and Nepal — I've noticed the same mistake repeat itself constantly.

Hiring managers conflate the two. And the confusion costs them.

## Defining the Terms (Properly)

**Employer-of-Record (EOR)** is a legal arrangement. A third-party company (the EOR provider) becomes the official employer of record for workers in a foreign country — handling payroll, taxes, benefits, labor law compliance, and termination risk. Your company controls the day-to-day work; the EOR handles the legal wrapper.

EOR makes sense when you want to hire an *individual* in a country where you don't have a legal entity. You use an EOR to avoid the cost and complexity of establishing a local subsidiary. Classic use case: a US company hiring a software engineer in Nepal without setting up a Nepali LLC.

**Technology-as-a-Service (TaaS)** is an *output model*. A vendor company provides access to pre-assembled teams, capabilities, or technology outcomes on a subscription or project basis. You're not hiring individuals — you're procuring a service that a team delivers. Contracts are typically B2B. IP arrangements, SLAs, and deliverables are the center of the relationship, not employment.

TaaS makes sense when you need a capability rather than a headcount. You want AI annotation done, or a research function built, or a content operation running — without managing each person individually.

**The confusion**: both models often involve people in other countries doing work for you. But legally, operationally, and contractually, they are completely different instruments.

## Why Companies Get This Wrong

The conflation happens at the point of need. A US company needs research work done. They think: "We need to hire someone in Nepal." They search for "EOR Nepal" — but what they actually want might be TaaS from a Nepali firm with research capacity.

Conversely, a company wanting full control over a specific individual they've already identified ends up signing a TaaS vendor contract — and then complaining when they can't direct the work the way they would an employee.

Three specific failure patterns I've documented in research:

**1. Compliance mismatch**: Companies use EOR when they should use TaaS (B2B contracts), resulting in misclassification risk if the person behaves more like an employee than a contractor. Or they use TaaS when they need EOR, leaving the individual worker without legal employment protections — which creates both ethical and regulatory exposure.

**2. Speed vs. control trade-off mismanaged**: EOR typically takes 1-4 weeks to set up per hire. TaaS can start in days. Companies that need speed but choose EOR pay a setup cost unnecessarily. Companies that want deep control but choose TaaS find that B2B service contracts don't give them the same authority over day-to-day work.

**3. IP and data ownership gaps**: In TaaS arrangements, IP is usually retained by the vendor unless explicitly transferred. In EOR arrangements, IP created by employees typically transfers to the employer. Companies that don't understand this distinction find themselves in disputes over work product.

## The Nepal Context

As someone based in Nepal and working in the R&D space, I've watched this play out from the other side of the transaction.

Nepal has a rapidly growing pool of research talent, data annotation capability, and tech workers. Companies in the US and Australia increasingly want access to this talent — but the models being used don't always serve either party well.

The EOR market in Nepal is growing, with providers now covering employment law compliance, provident fund contributions (EPF), and Social Security Fund (SSF) obligations. This makes Nepal a viable EOR destination — but only if the hiring company understands what EOR actually delivers.

The TaaS market is even more interesting. Nepali firms offering research, AI data services, content, and tech capabilities on a service basis represent genuine leverage for global companies — faster engagement, lower overhead, and access to domain expertise. But the model requires trust, clear SLAs, and buyers who understand B2B vendor relationships.

## A Simple Decision Framework

When deciding between TaaS and EOR, ask three questions:

**1. Do you want this specific person, or this specific output?**
- Specific person → EOR
- Specific output → TaaS

**2. Do you need to control how the work is done, or just verify what's delivered?**
- Control the process → EOR (employee relationship)
- Verify outcomes → TaaS (vendor relationship)

**3. Are you scaling a team or accessing a capability?**
- Building a team → EOR, with long-term employment intent
- Accessing a capability on demand → TaaS, with contract flexibility

Most situations have a clear answer once these questions are on the table. The failure happens when companies skip this diagnosis and go straight to vendor search.

## Conclusion

The global talent market is genuinely rewiring how companies access skills and capabilities. TaaS and EOR are both powerful tools — but they're not interchangeable. Understanding which one fits your situation is a compliance question, a strategy question, and increasingly, a competitive question.

Companies that get this right move faster, stay compliant, and build better remote teams. Companies that conflate the models pay the cost in misaligned contracts, unhappy workers, and regulatory headaches.

For remote workers and vendors in markets like Nepal, the implication is also clear: know which model your engagement falls under, and make sure your agreements reflect it. The informal "we'll figure it out" arrangement that worked when remote work was rare will not hold up at scale.
